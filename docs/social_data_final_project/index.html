1. Introduction and understanding the data.

We decided to analyze the dataset from the trees of the city of Melbourne. We thought it would be interesting because it is something that is very easily overlook. When walking into a park or down the street people tend not to appreciate the green. Those trees are the equivalent of a natural cooling system.

What is the total size of your data? (MB, number of rows, number of variables, etc)

The size of the dataset is 14.8 Mb Urban_forest_in_Mebourne. There are 74.6K rows, each row representing a tree and 19 columns. The columns we decided to use in our analysis are mostly the’Common Name' of the tree, the ‘Family’ that the tree belongs to , the’Diameter Breast Height', the ‘Date Planted’ and the ‘Located in’ for the machine learning.

What are other properties? (What is the date range? Is is it geo-data?, then a quick plot of locations, etc.)

There are geo-data in the dataset (‘Latitude’, ‘Longitude’) that we are planning to use for heat-map representation. We are also going to incorporate the ‘Life Expectancy’ column so that the life expectancy is exceeded, the tree will disappear from the map.

3.Random Forest Classification

We will choose the two most frequent families of trees and use the random forest classifier machine learning technique. The classifier will will take the diameter at breast height and the life expectancy of a tree and return the familly of the tree.

Diameter at breast height (otherwise known as DBH), is the standard for measuring trees. It refers to the tree diameter measured at 4.5 feet (approximately 1,4 meters) above the ground.

As mentioned earlier, columns Diameter Breast Height and Useful Life Expectancy have high rates of missing values. Because of the volume of the dataset, when dropping the missing values, we still had large amount of data to train and test our model.
